While there are purely algebraic proofs of the Nielsen-Schreier theorem, a much easier proof is available through geometric group theory.

Let $G$ be a group which is free on a set $X$.  Any group acts freely on its Cayley graph, and the Cayley graph of $G$ is a $2|X|$-regular tree, which we will call $\mathcal{T}$.

If $H$ is any subgroup of $G$, then $H$ also acts freely on $\mathcal{T}$ by restriction.  Since groups that act freely on trees are free, $H$ is free.

Moreover, we can obtain the rank of $H$ (the size of the set on which it is free).  If $\mathcal{G}$ is a finite graph, then $\pi_1(\mathcal{G})$ is free of rank $-\chi(\mathcal{G})-1$, where $\chi(\mathcal{G})$ denotes the Euler characteristic of $\mathcal{G}$.  Since $H\cong\pi_1(H\backslash\mathcal{T})$, the rank of $H$ is $\chi(H\backslash\mathcal{T})$.  If $H$ is of finite index $n$ in $G$, then $H\backslash\mathcal{T}$ is finite, and $\chi(H\backslash\mathcal{T})=n\chi(G\backslash\mathcal{T})$.  Of course $-\chi(G\backslash\mathcal{T})+1$ is the rank of $G$.  Substituting, we obtain the Schreier index formula: $$\mathrm{rank}(H)=n(\mathrm{rank}(G)-1)+1.$$
