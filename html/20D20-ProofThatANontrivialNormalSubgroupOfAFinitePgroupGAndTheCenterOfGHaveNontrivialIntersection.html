proof that a nontrivial normal subgroup of a finite p-group G and the center of G have nontrivial intersection


Define G to act on H by conjugation; that is, for g∈G, h∈H, define

g⋅h=g⁢h⁢g-1

Note that g⋅h∈H since H◁G. This is easily seen to be a well-defined group action.

Now, the set of invariants of H under this action are

GH={h∈H|g⋅h=h∀g∈G}={h∈H|ghg-1=h∀g∈G}=H∩Z(G)

The class equation theorem states that

|H|=|GH|+∑i=1r[G:Gxi]

where the Gxi are proper subgroups of G, and thus that

|GH|=|H|-∑i=1r[G:Gxi]

We now use elementary group theory to show that p divides each term on the right, and conclude as a result that p divides |GH|, so that GH=H∩Z⁢(G) cannot be trivial.

As G is a nontrivial finite p-group, it is obvious from Cauchy’s theorem that |G|=pn for n>0. Since H and the Gxi are subgroups of G, each either is trivial or has order a power of p, by Lagrange’s theorem. Since H is nontrivial, its order is a nonzero power of p. Since each Gxi is a proper subgroup of G and has order a power of p, it follows that [G:Gxi] also has order a nonzero power of p.
