example of infinite simple group


This fact that finite alternating groups are simple can be extended to a result about an infinite group. Let G be the subgroup of the group of permutations on a countably infinite set M (which we may take to be the set of natural numbers for concreteness) which is generated by cycles of length 3. Note that any since every element of this group is a product of a finite number of cycles, the permutations of G are such that only a finite number of elements of our set are not mapped to themselves by a given permutation.

We will now show that G is simple. Suppose that π is an element of G other than the identity. Let m be the set of all x such that π⁢(x)≠x. By our previous comment, m is finite. Consider the restriction πm of π to m. By the theorem of the parent entry (http://planetmath.org/SimplicityOfA_n), the subgroup of Am generated by the conjugates of πm is the whole of Am. In particular, this means that there exists a cycle of order 3 in Am which can be expressed as a product of πm and its conjugates. Hence the subgroup of G generated by conjugates of π contains a cycle of length three as well. However, every cycle of order 3 is conjugate to every other cycle of order 3 so, in fact, the subgroup of G generated by the conjugates of π is the whole of G. Hence, the only normal subgroups of G are the group consisting of solely the identity element and the whole of G, so G is a simple group.
